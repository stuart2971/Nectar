daikon radish

Like the sparker white tip radish, these roots love water.  They are pretty much fool-proof and can be grown by anyone and anywhere.  Since daikon radish are the much larger than most others, they may take a longer time to mature.  

These radish actually prefer to grow in colder climates, so if you live in a place where it is cooler weather most of the year it may be more beneficial for you.  

Since the size of this radish is so large, many farmers use it to loosen u the soil for their other crops, which also reduces soil erosion.  

According to other gardeners that have experience in growing daikon radish, they say that growing this type is better when using a seed, rather than roots from another radish.  Next year, I will try both and let you guys know what I prefer!

When it comes to nutrients, DO NOT USE NITROGEN.  At least, do not add extra nitrogen to the soil.  Nitrogen is mainly used in other plants for making leaves bigger and the roots smaller.  Since the part we eat is the roots, using nitrogen will just make our radishes smaller.  

The germination process is very fast, as it can only take a few days for the seeds to sprout.  The soil should be constantly moist.  If the roots start to appear above ground, do not mind it, that is a sign that they will grow to be a very large radish.  

Try to keep the soil as loose as you can to allow the radish to get as large as possible.  You can do this by:

1. Use a fork - You can try to use a fork or a stick to loosen the soil every so often, but these may not be long enough to reach the soil under the radish.  
2. Use a soil mix - Before even the germinating process, mix in different types of material that can loosen soil, such as mulch, sand, wood chips, etc.  

Depending on the density of the soil, the shape and size of the radish may change.  The 3 main categories are:
1. Oblong
2. Long with pointed end
3. Round

Bug will typically target the leaves.  If you wish to preserve them, maybe try planting them near other natural insect repellant plants, such as basil.  To avoid insect infestations, be sure to plant the radish far apart.  

To harvest, the easiest way is to poke around the root of the plant and gently pull the radish out of the ground.  If you notice that the radish tip has snapped (occurs most in the long with pointed tip shaped radish), there really is not much you can do other than keep poking at it with tool, such as a fork.

Storage is pretty much the same as any other type of radish.
- Be sure not to  clean then until you’re ready to eat it  
- Keep them in a cool, dry place to avoid molding  
- The radish leaves can be preserved in an air tight baggie or container
- Pickling the radish may result in longer storage time
